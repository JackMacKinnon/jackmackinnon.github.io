WATonomous

Vehicle Dynamics/Feedback Controller - 09/17-12/18

WATonomous is the University of Waterloo's team in GM's AutoDrive Challenge, hosted by SAE. WATonomous is converting a 2017 Chevrolet Bolt into a fully autonomous vehicle.

I spent a year and four months with WATonomous and I focused on the Vehicle Dynamics/Feedback controller of the Bolt. Over the course of four terms (4 months/term), I held the positions of "Core Member", "Sub-Team Lead", "Core Member" and "Mentor".

The responsibility of Vehicle Dynamics/Feedback controller is to determine the trajectory of the vehicle as it follows an inputted local path. A local path is a series of vehicle states (x, y, heading) that determine the state of the vehicle for the near future. In other words, we're handed a vector of Path_Points which specify an x, y and heading for each state along a 2D plot (think birds-eye-view of the vehicle). With the first point containing information about the current velocity and acceleration, and the last point containing information about the requested final velocity and acceleration, it is up to us to fill in the details; point-to-point. We also have competition constraints on the maximum and minimum values of velocity, acceleration and jerk to abide by.

After working out details with other sub-teams, we came to a conclusion about the algorithmic approach we should take to map out the details. We decided on using a recursive function to traverse the vector, assigning a "maximum-value" attribute to jerk and working backwards. We find acceleration and velocity by integrating over the time taken between points. Once these values are determined, we check to make sure we haven't broken any constraints. If we have, we reduce the jerk and try again. If we can't find a jerk value that satisfies motion for the vehicle state, we return to the previous point and reduce that jerk and try again.

That's just a quick and dirty summary of the kind of work I did with WATonomous. I'm sure it was a little confusing, so contact me and we can work through the details!

Autonomous Checkers-Playing Robot

Project Manager - 10/17-12/17

At the end of my 1A term my class was assigned a small design project. Using a LEGO MINDSTORM, design a robotic system.

Very general, very broad. Three classmates and I teamed up and begun to brainstorm different projects we could do. What did we settle on? An autonomous checkers-playing robot, naturally.

Our design leveraged a 3D grid system to scan and traverse the board (think 3D printer). We packaged everything required to move around the board within a "carriage". This carriage housed two color sensors, a motor to drive across the board and an electromagnet. Did I mention we chose to grab pieces by using an electromagnet? This whole carriage rested on a plank of wood that in-tern, rested on a wooden frame. Two motors powered larger wheels on the frame to move the entire wooden plank with the carriage.

I took on the role of programming the system's movement. This includes making measurements of the board and other features to store in the memory of the robot. Movements span from driving the robot across the board, to descending and ascending the electromagnet, to supplying power to and from the electromagnet to engage and disengage the pieces.
